Demonstrations

The Wolfram Demonstrations site is really cool. It has thousands of programs just like the CDFs found on this site written on all sorts of subjects. These demonstrations are interactive ways of presenting simple and advanced mathematical concepts alike (among other things) in an approachable and attractive manner. They are all accompanied by written explanations and links to other useful sources. Plus, anybody can contribute their own programs, so if you have Mathematica and have something you’d like to share, you can submit your own demonstration to Wolfram’s site. But otherwise, it’s a really interesting site to check out, so you should certainly do it right now.

 

Here are the demonstrations that I have contributed to the Wolfram Demonstration site. If you like the CDFs that are all over this site, then you’ll like these (they’re generally even more complex) and are bound to like the thousands of others you can find here.

 

Orbital Parameters

This demo illustrates the variables that are used to position a satellite and it’s orbit around a central body.  It also has the orbits and up-to-date positions of the Earth relative to the sun and the Moon relative to the Earth built in.

 

 

The Physics of Flight

This demo simulates the flight of an aircraft, allowing you to control the variables that influence lift, drag, thrust, and weight.

 

 

 

Gray Code’s Error Reduction with Encoders

Learn the difference between traditional binary code and Gray code and why it can be a better choice in some scenarios.

 

 

Dropping a Test Charge in an Electric Field

Create a complex electric field from multiple stationary charges and then drop a mobile charge into it to see how it behaves. This demo also provides an intuitive way to visualize complicated fields using a contour plot.

 

 

 

Downbursts and the Moody Diagram

Downbursts are meteorological phenomena create by derechos that are capable of massive damage. Find out which variables have a great influence on the violence of the storm using physics borrowed from pipe mechanics.
